## Introduction

Artificial Intelligence is overhyped. However, there is no denying that it’s a new frontier of technology that should be completely explored before being dismissed. Unfortunately, companies like OpenAI, Google, Meta, Anthropic, etc. are all exploring the wrong avenues of innovation. Allow me to share my vision of the future regarding AI.

## Chapter 1: Evil Data Centers

Right now, the primary way that people interface with large language models (LLMs) is through a web GUI that hits an API endpoint at some massive, centralized data center. This approach, while functional, is incredibly crude and is frankly awful in essentially every way. Data centers as a whole, but especially the GPU-based facilities required for modern AI, are a monument to inefficiency, guzzling resources at a rate that is environmentally, economically, and practically unsustainable. This model of centralized AI is not just a temporary inconvenience; it is a foundational flaw that threatens the long-term viability and equitable distribution of this transformative technology.

The most immediate and alarming issue is the staggering environmental toll. These AI data centers absolutely guzzle power, with a single training run for a model like GPT-5 consuming as much electricity as hundreds of homes for an entire year. This immense energy demand is often met by fossil fuels, directly contributing to carbon emissions and accelerating climate change. Beyond electricity, there is the often-overlooked crisis of water consumption. GPUs generate immense heat and require constant cooling, which is typically achieved through water-based evaporation systems. A single data center can consume millions of gallons of water per day, a reckless extravagance in an era of increasing global water scarcity. We are, in effect, trading precious natural resources for the ability to generate robotic text and tired images, a transaction with dangerously skewed costs and benefits.

Economically, the centralized model is just as precarious. The capital expenditure required to build a state-of-the-art AI data center runs into the billions of dollars, a cost dominated by the procurement of tens of thousands of specialized GPUs. This immense financial barrier ensures that only a handful of trillion-dollar corporations can compete, creating a dangerous concentration of power and stifling innovation. The operational expenditures are similarly daunting, with electricity bills alone reaching tens of millions of dollars annually. This is not a sustainable economic model for a technology meant to be ubiquitous; it’s a gilded cage, accessible only to a select few, and it creates a dynamic of dependency where the future of AI is dictated not by a community of innovators, but by the boardrooms of a few tech giants.

Finally, from a practical and societal standpoint, this reliance on centralized infrastructure is a strategic blunder. It creates a fragile system with massive single points of failure, vulnerable to outages, cyberattacks, and even geopolitical pressures. Furthermore, it forces users to send their data to a third-party server, creating significant privacy and security risks. The current model is building a future where access to powerful AI is a utility controlled by a few, who will inevitably mediate our relationship with the technology. This is not a recipe for a democratized, human-centric technological revolution; it’s the blueprint for a digital oligarchy. The path forward cannot be to simply build more of these unsustainable behemoths. So then, what is the path forward?

## Chapter 2: On-Device AI

There is not a shred of doubt in my mind that on-device AI, specifically for LLMs, is the way forward. The model of outsourcing our computational needs to a remote, power-hungry monolith is a dead end. The future is not in the cloud, it's in your hand. This concept isn't even new, we've been using on-device machine learning for years without thinking about it. When your phone's photo gallery automatically identifies and groups pictures of your friends and family, that's on-device ML. When your keyboard suggests the next word in your sentence, or your camera app adjusts the focus and aperture in real-time, that is all happening locally on your device's processor. We have already accepted and integrated this form of AI into our daily lives because it is seamless, fast, and secure.

The benefits of this local-first approach are monumental. The most profound impact would be environmental. Shifting from cloud-based to on-device inference isn't a minor tweak, it's a necessary, ethical shift in energy consumption. Processing an AI task locally can be anywhere from 100 to 1,000 times more energy-efficient than sending it to a data center. This approach eliminates the energy overhead of network transmission, massive cooling systems, and the constant idle power draw of server racks. It means the environmental cost of an AI query drops from a transaction that strains power grids and depletes water resources to one that uses a tiny sip of your device's battery. Widespread adoption would represent one of the single largest potential reductions in the tech industry's carbon footprint.

This local-first system also unlocks the true potential of a personal assistant: personalization. The pairing of a small, efficient model with a specialized inference ASIC (much like the Neural Processing Units (NPUs) already in modern phones) creates a secure sandbox for the AI to learn from its user. It can adapt to your unique vocabulary, writing style, and personal context without ever sending that sensitive information to a corporate server. This opens the door for a truly bespoke AI, one that can anticipate your needs because it understands you. The ultimate form of this, which we will explore in Chapter 4, is a hypothetical framework for self-fine-tuning, where the local model continuously and privately adapts to become a more useful and intuitive partner in your daily tasks.

Making this vision a reality would require a fundamental shift in the industry's focus. For the last several years, the race has been to build bigger and bigger models, with ever-increasing parameter counts, chasing marginal gains on benchmark tests. This is the wrong race to be running. The true innovation lies not in creating ever-smarter models, but in creating smaller, more efficient models. The goal should be to distill the capabilities of a 100-billion-parameter model into a 1-billion-parameter model that can run efficiently on a consumer device. This is a challenge of algorithmic optimization and model architecture, not just brute-force scaling. We need to prioritize ingenuity over sheer size.

Crucially, this switch is already possible for a vast majority of consumer tasks. The small models that exist today are more than capable of handling common requests like drafting an email, summarizing a document, or scheduling a meeting. The relentless pursuit of massive, data-center-bound models is only necessary for the most complex, niche problems like high-level, theoretical mathematics or advanced computer science, capabilities that the average user simply does not need. By focusing on optimizing the capable tools we already have for the devices we already own, we can pave the way for a powerful, personal AI that is secure, efficient, and accessible to everyone.

## Chapter 3: Inference ASICs

For those unfamiliar, there are two steps to machine learning models: training and inference. Training is the process of feeding the model data and improving it based on its response to that data. If the model is a cake, training is baking the cake in the oven. Inference is the process of running the model once it has been trained. When you talk to ChatGPT, that is inference. An ASIC is an application-specific integrated circuit. Just think of it as a processor that is really really good at a specific task, but can’t do much else. I think you might see where I’m going with this. If we can build an ASIC for high-efficiency AI inference, then on-device AI would take over. Now, at this point, you may be asking: “Why can’t we just make ASICs for both inference and training, and squash the need for these wallet-abusing Nvidia GPUs?” That’s an excellent question, and the answer is a little complicated.

First off, let's cover why you can’t make a single ASIC that does both training and inference. Training and inference, while somewhat similar processes, are mathematically different enough to kill this idea. Training is a two-way street. It involves a "forward pass," which is identical to inference, where data is fed through the neural network to get an output. But then it requires a "backward pass," or backpropagation, where the model calculates the error in its output and sends that error signal backward through the network to adjust its internal parameters, or weights. This process requires storing all the intermediate calculations from the forward pass to be used in the backward pass, making it incredibly memory-intensive. Furthermore, these adjustments are often tiny, requiring high-precision mathematics (like 32-bit floating-point numbers, or FP32) to be calculated accurately.

Inference, on the other hand, is a one-way street. It's only the forward pass. Data goes in, an answer comes out, and the model's weights are never changed. This simplicity is a massive advantage. Because you don't need to store intermediate values for a backward pass, the memory requirements are far lower. More importantly, you can get away with much lower numerical precision. A trained model can often be "quantized," converting its high-precision weights into low-precision 8-bit (or lower) integers with minimal loss in accuracy. The computational workload for an 8-bit integer is a fraction of that for a 32-bit floating-point number, leading to a dramatic reduction in power consumption and a massive increase in speed. An ASIC built for inference can be hardwired to do one thing perfectly: low-precision, high-speed matrix multiplication. An ASIC for training would need to handle two fundamentally different computational workloads and manage a much larger and more complex memory architecture. You'd end up with a chip that does both jobs poorly.

This leads to the second, more practical problem: the pace of innovation. The field of AI is in constant flux. New model architectures and training techniques emerge monthly. A GPU, for all its power-hungry flaws, is a general-purpose parallel computer. It’s flexible. When a new algorithm comes out, you can write new software (using a framework like Nvidia’s CUDA) to run it on existing GPUs. An ASIC is the opposite of flexible; it’s extremely rigid, with the algorithm literally etched into the silicon. If you spend a billion dollars and two years designing a "training ASIC" for today's state-of-the-art model, it is almost guaranteed to be obsolete by the time it's manufactured. The risk is astronomical. Inference, however, is a much more stable target. While models change, the fundamental task of running a forward pass on a trained neural network remains the same, making it a perfect candidate for the hyper-specialization of an ASIC.

At this point, you’re probably saying “Wow this sounds like an amazing idea! Why isn’t anyone doing it?” Well, someone actually is. It’s a relatively small, private company called “Groq” (not to be confused with xAI’s “Grok”, no relation). They make something called an “LPU” or Language Processing Unit which is basically an inference ASIC. So then if it’s such a good idea, and someone is already doing it, why haven’t you heard of Groq and why haven’t they taken over the AI inference market? Well, there isn’t a clear answer to that question, so I can only give my personal opinion. Personally, I think its because of their business model and target customer base. They primarily market to companies and focus on huge rack-mounted deployments, similar to an Nvidia rack deployment that people are much more familiar with. I think this is a massive waste, and they should be focusing on smaller, more efficient on-device processors like we discussed in chapter 2.

## Chapter 4: Self Fine-Tuning

The combination of small, efficient models and purpose-built inference hardware doesn't just solve the problems of the evil data center, it unlocks the holy grail of AI: true, continuous personalization. The static, one-size-fits-all models of today will be replaced by dynamic, bespoke assistants that evolve with their users. This can be achieved through a framework of automated, on-device self-fine-tuning, a process that is both technically feasible and essential for making AI a genuinely useful personal tool.

First, let's clear up any confusion surrounding fine-tuning. It's the process of taking a pre-trained "base" model and training it a little more on a smaller, specialized dataset. This is an extremely common procedure. It’s not about teaching the model a new language, but about teaching it _your_ language. Think of the base model as a brilliant university graduate with vast general knowledge. Fine-tuning is the on-the-job training that makes them an expert in a specific role. Companies do this constantly, fine-tuning a base model like Llama 3 on their internal documents, emails, and codebases to create a specialized version that understands the company's specific context and jargon. This process has been democratized by (semi) open-source tools like Unsloth, which leverage techniques like Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA). Instead of re-training the billions of parameters of the base model, LoRA freezes the original model and inserts tiny, trainable "adapter" layers. This drastically reduces the memory and compute power needed, making fine-tuning accessible outside of massive research labs.

The framework I envision takes this a step further, from the corporate level to the individual, through a continuous, automated, and completely **private** cycle.
### Section 1: Secure, Passive Data Curation

The process begins with the on-device model passively observing user activity within a secure, OS-level (NOT kernel-level) sandbox. This is the critical privacy preserving step. The raw data, the emails you write, the code you develop, the notes you take, never leaves your device and is never seen by any third party. The system isn't logging keystrokes, it's identifying high-quality "cause and effect" pairs. For example, it might note the initial prompt you gave the AI, ”summarize this report for an executive”, and the final version of the summary you actually sent after making your own edits. Your edits become the ultimate truth, the ideal response the model should have given.

## Stage 2: Automated Pruning and Formatting

Raw data is messy. The second stage involves an automated process, running during device idle time, that prunes this curated data. It filters out duplicates, low-quality interactions, and personal data that isn't relevant for improving the model's utility. The goal is to distill the raw observations into a clean, high-quality dataset of instruction-response pairs. For instance, the system would format the previous example into a training sample: 
```json
{"instruction": "summarize this report for an executive", "input": "[full report text]", "output": "[your final edited summary]"}
``` 
This clean dataset is the bible for the model's personalized education.

## Stage 3: Lightweight, Idle-Time Fine-Tuning

This is where the magic happens. When your device is idle and charging (likely overnight), the system initiates a lightweight fine-tuning process using the newly formatted dataset and the device's specialized NPU. Leveraging techniques like LoRA, it updates the small adapter layers without touching the massive core model. This is computationally inexpensive and energy-efficient, akin to a software update happening in the background. Each cycle makes the model incrementally better, nudging its responses to be more aligned with your specific needs, style, and context.

This continuous loop creates an AI assistant that becomes exponentially more useful over time. This framework would be incredibly valuable to the average consumer as a personal assistant. However, it would likely be even more valuable to companies looking to provide assistants to it’s employees. Right now, the industry standard is for a company to either run their own fine-tuned model on their own servers or pay exorbitant API costs to have a third party run it. Instead, companies could use this hypothetical framework and have their fine-tuned model run on each employees work-issued laptop and/or phone, which would result in essentially zero up-keep costs (besides the occasional tech-support tickets that companies already deal with).

It would learn not only the company's acronyms and products but also that specific employee's role, their communication style, and the context of their current work. It would know that when you say "draft a project update," you prefer a bulleted list and a direct tone. It will be able to summarize a technical document by pulling out the specific details that are relevant to _your_ role on the team. It becomes an expert on both the company and the individual, an intelligent helper that anticipates needs rather than just reacting to commands. This entire system is only possible with the foundation laid in the previous chapters: small models that are cheap to update, running on efficient, purpose-built processors. It's the logical end-game for personal computing, a truly intelligent assistant, shaped by and for the individual user.

It should be noted that this is a rough idea for what a hypothetical framework _could_ look like. This is not a be-all, end-all solution.

## Chapter 5: The Lies of AGI

“C’mon bro, we’re so close to AGI bro, it’s gonna solve all of our problems, c’mon I just need $100 billion more dollars bro please”. That’s my impression of Sam Altman to whoever holds economic power at that specific time (especially the government). I’m just gonna rip the bandaid off now: Not only are we no where near AGI, but even if we invented it today, not much would change. It’s really only kept alive by these AI giants so they can use it as their Moby Dick to keep building more and more data centers. The goal is to keep chasing this smoke and mirrors entity, gathering more investments and subsidies, until they’re too big to fail. Some of you may be doubtful of the accuracy of my position, and I don’t blame you. But to really see the bigger picture, you need to take a step back, and pull yourself out of the bubble.

The hype is deafening, and every new model release is framed as another leap towards this god-like intelligence. But this narrative fundamentally misunderstands how technological progress works. It isn't an endless, exponential rocket ride to infinity. Every technological revolution in history has followed an S-curve: a slow start, a period of rapid, explosive growth, and then a leveling off as the technology matures and hits fundamental limits. We saw it with the steam engine, we saw it with electricity, and we are seeing it with the very processors that power this AI boom.

For fifty years, the tech industry was defined by Moore's Law, the observation that the number of transistors on a chip doubled roughly every two years. This reliable, exponential growth was the engine of the digital age. But that engine is sputtering. We are now hitting the hard physical limits of silicon. Transistors are approaching the size of atoms, and the costs of making them smaller are becoming astronomical. The curve is flattening. The idea that AI progress is somehow immune to these same realities is absurd. We’re in the steep, exciting part of the AI S-curve right now, where every new model feels like a revolution. But the low-hanging fruit is being picked, and the returns from simply throwing more data and more GPUs at the problem are diminishing. We are not on an exponential path to AGI; we are on a logistical curve hurtling towards a plateau, and no amount of venture capital can change that.

Let's indulge the fantasy for a moment and say they succeed. AGI is born tomorrow in a server rack in Texas. What then? Does the world magically change? Of course not. An all-knowing intelligence is useless without the ability to act in the physical world, a problem robotics is decades, maybe centuries, away from solving. And even if it could act, who would let it? The entire concept is a PR nightmare. Anyone who’s seen _2001: A Space Odyssey_ has a deep, instinctual distrust of a disembodied, all-powerful AI (and for good reason). The idea that we would just hand over the keys to our economy, our infrastructure, and/or our military to a real-life HAL 9000 is laughable. The trust simply isn't there, and it never will be. AGI isn't a savior; it's a solution in search of a problem, and a boogeyman that's great for securing funding. Our society is human-first at its core, and it should stay that way.

## Conclusion

To reiterate, there is no doubt in my mind that on-device, private, efficient, self-fine-tuning Large Language Models are the future of humanity’s relationship with consumer AI. In other fields, like video engineering, the story is a little different and, in a lot of ways, more interesting and uncertain. However, that is beyond the scope of this piece. Sooner or later, the evil data centers of today will become the evil data centers of yesteryear, and it will certainly not be a mournful day.
